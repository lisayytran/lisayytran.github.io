All About DNA!

What is DNA?

Deoxyribonucleic Acid (DNA) is a molecule that carries all of our genetic information. Think of it as the coding responsible for the genetic makeup that gives a person a specific characteristic. It contains all the information needed for a living organism to grow and function. DNA is hereditary, meaning it passes from each parent to child. Despite this, every person's DNA is unique!

Fun Fact: Only the DNA of identical twins are not unique! This is because they come from a single fertilised egg that splits into two

The Structure of DNA

DNA consists of a double helix structure, often described as two long strands arranged in a spiral. These two strands consist of nucleotides that run in opposite directions. Each nucleotide is comprised of a phosphate group, a sugar molecule, and a nitrogenous base. There are four types of nitrogenous bases in DNA: Adenine (A), Thymine (T), Cytosine (C), and Guanine (G). These bases always pair up in a specific way: Adenine pairs with Thymine (A-T), and Guanine pairs with Cytosine (G-C). This complimentary base pairing is held together by hydrogen bonds.

Fun Fact: There are over 3 billion different base pairs in each person!

Organisation of DNA

DNA is organised differently in different types of organisms. This can be divided into two main groups, eukaryotes and prokaryotes. Animals and plants are eukaryotes. In eukaryotes, DNA can be found within the chromosomes inside a nucleus, a membrane-bound compartment within a cell. The DNA in the nucleus is tightly coiled and packaged with proteins. Bacteria is an example of a prokaryote. Unlike eukaryotes, these organisms do not have a nucleus within their cells which means their DNA is free in the cytoplasm. Prokaryotic DNA is found in circular chromosomes but can also be found in smaller loops called plasmids.
